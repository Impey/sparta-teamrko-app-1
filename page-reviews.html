Horror:
Hallowen

In his brilliant turn-of-the-century documentary The American Nightmare, Adam Simon located John Carpenter’s 1978 Halloween as the end point of a decade of countercultural horror movies. Starting with George Romero’s 1968 Night of the Living Dead, Simon unpicked the rebellious socio-political threads of films such as Last House on the Left, The Texas Chain Saw Massacre and Shivers before arriving at the more conservative inflections of Carpenter’s ruthlessly efficient modern morality tale. A stylishly suspenseful thriller in which teenagers indulging in illicit sex and intoxication are stalked and slashed by a relentless killer, Halloween was a funhouse ride with a puritanical narrative edge. (“I didn’t mean to put an end to the sexual revolution,” Carpenter laughingly told Simon, “and for that I deeply apologise.”) Yet it also had a punky power that inspired a slew of titillating teen-terror slashers. Friday the 13th may have lifted its gory riffs from Mario Bava’s A Bay of Blood (just as Carpenter looked back to Bob Clark’s Black Christmas), but it was Halloween that paved the way for its blockbuster success. A string of inferior sequels and reboots followed Carpenter’s low-budget hit, from Rick Rosenthal’s 1981 Halloween II (written by original screenwriters Carpenter and Debra Hill) to Rob Zombie’s dreary 2007 “reimagining” and its dispiriting follow-up. Now David Gordon Green, whose career has swerved from Terrence Malick-esque indie-hit George Washington to the stoner comedy of Pineapple Express and Your Highness, retreads familiar ground with added PTSD. Like the 20th-anniversary outing Halloween H20, which effectively overrode instalments four to six (the superior Halloween III: Season of the Witch follows an entirely separate narrative), this latest incarnation effectively dumps the sequels and simply picks up 40 years after the events of the first film, with surprisingly sprightly results. Meanwhile, survivor and “final-girl” archetype Laurie Strode (Jamie Lee Curtis, terrific) is living in a remote home-cum-security-compound. Haunted by the murderous spectre of Myers (aka “the shape”), Laurie’s paranoia has taken its toll; she’s seen her marriages collapse, become estranged from her daughter, Karen (a sharply cast Judy Greer), who was taken away as a child, and now fears for the safety of her teenage granddaughter Allyson (Andi Matichak). As Laurie tells the annoying podcasters who show up looking for a story: “I’m a basket case.” When Michael escapes during transfer to a maximum security prison (a nod to Halloween 4?), Laurie is hardly surprised. “He’s waited for me,” she says. “I’ve waited for him…”

Comedy:
Step Brothers

Another unashamedly juvenile comedy from the awe-inspiringly busy Judd Apatow production line: Apatow produces, and the director is Adam McKay, who made the very funny Anchorman: The Legend of Ron Burgundy. Will Ferrell and John C Reilly play two 40-year-old guys who live with their respective single parents and have regressed, horribly, to being 13 (a parable, some would say, for the people who make these movies and those who enjoy them). When the boys' long-suffering parents (Richard Jenkins and Mary Steenburgen) happen to meet, fall in love at a medical-technology conference and then get married, Ferrell and Reilly have to share a bedroom as stepbrothers. There are certainly one or two funny, broad moments, especially when the "boys" get it into their heads to convert their beds into bunk beds, and their impersonation of 13-year-olds is scarily accurate. But Reilly has always been more actor than comic, and this goofy comedy is a DVD rental only.

Action:
The Expendables

Stallone! Statham! Willis! Li! Lundgren! Rourke! Schwarzenegger! The lineup makes it sound like it's set in the world's most macho prostate clinic. Sylvester Stallone has called in some favours and created an old-school, pre-Bourne action movie, featuring only pampered beefcakes of a certain age: spiritually located Where Eagles Dare to Fumble for Their Reading Glasses. Sly plays Barney Ross, a cigar-smoking tough guy who heads up a crew of golden-hearted mercenaries. But wait. If you're a Bruce Willis fan, or an Arnie fan, and you're expecting your heroes to have equal screen time with the rest – think again. Willis and Governor Schwarzenegger only get the briefest of cameos at the beginning, but their unsuspecting fanbases will pump up the box office receipts. He's not called "Sly" for nothing, you know: if it was just a diminutive, he'd be "Syl". Anyway, Barney and his compadres are paid to fly in to "Vilena", a Spanish-speaking island somewhere in the "Gulf" and topple a military dictatorship, without worrying over much about leaving behind a stable infrastructure. Poor old Jet Li is mightily dissed with loads of "Chinese people are short" jokes in this lumbering, humourless film, and he never gets to win a single punch-up.
